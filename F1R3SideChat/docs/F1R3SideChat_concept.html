Victoria found the second glove at half past midnight, tucked behind the false panel in the billiard room—the one Whitmore himself had shown her three years ago, before he understood what she was. The leather was still damp. Someone had been wearing it recently, or had wanted her to think so.

She lit the second lamp and held both gloves up against the window, comparing the seams.

Balthazar appeared in the doorway, his coat still carrying the smell of the river. He did not ask about the gloves. He watched her, and something in his stillness said he had already worked it out—or thought he had.

"You'll want to look at the cufflinks next," he said, at last. "The pattern is a maker's mark. Flemish, probably. I know a man in Antwerp."

The Inspector cleared his throat from the corridor outside, loudly enough that they would know he had been there some time. He stepped in without being invited—his usual manner—and registered the two lamps, the gloves, and Balthazar's river-damp coat with one slow sweep of his eyes.

"The household staff are asleep, Miss Alderton. Or so they claim." He did not look at her when he said it. He was looking at the gloves. "Someone ought to tell them that innocent people seldom need to claim anything."
